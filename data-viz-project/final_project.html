Young voters double voting rate from 2014

By: Colleen Neely

A surprising increase from 2014

The 2018 midterm elections were historic for several reasons. A record number of women were voted to Congress. A “blue wave” of candidates gave Democrats a majority in the House of Representatives. And now, a Tufts University study found that the voting rate among college students doubled in 2018 from 2014.

The National Study of Learning, Voting, and Engagement used the deidentified records of over 10 million students from more than 1,000 colleges and universities who chose to participate, including the University of Maryland, College Park.

The study, published in September 2019, reported that the college student voting rate went from 19% in 2014 to 40% in 2018.

“College and university students today are more diverse than ever, and while they are not a monolithic group, the 20 million students enrolled in U.S. colleges and universities present a formidable voting bloc,” wrote Nancy Thomas, director of the Institute for Democracy & Higher Education at Tufts University.

College student voter turnout compared to overall 18-24 year old voter turnout

Why did this happen?

The mere increase in turnout was not surprising given overall voter turnout increased by 11 percentage points, according to the Census Bureau. What was surprising, however, was how dramatic the increase in college voter turnout was, the study said.

Because the increase was so dramatic, researchers concluded it was likely due to a number of factors including multiple hot-button issues, high stakes races, and campus energy.

Additionally, “alarming low voting rates in 2012 and 2014 were a wake-up call to many individual campuses and to the higher education community broadly,” Thomas said.

Voter turnout by state in 2018 among 18-24 year olds

A closer look: trends at the University of Maryland, College Park

This university also saw a similar increase in voter turnout on campus. In 2014, 19.3% of University of Maryland students voted. In 2018, campus turnout increased to 46.0%, according to a separate report, also released last September.

The 2018 voting rate at this university was six percentage points higher than the national college student voting rate.

Further, researchers found that 41.1% of undergraduates and 36.4% of graduate students at this university voted in the 2018 election.

Voter turnout among women at this university was 10 percentage points higher than that of men.

Voting methods used at the University of Maryland, College Park in 2014 and 2018

“In 2018, we saw new energy and a greater sense of agency among students that transcends demographic and disciplinary subgroups,” Thomas said.

While the voting rate for 18-24 year olds as well as college students was still less than 50%, the upward trend is encouraging and may indicate a continued increase in future elections.
